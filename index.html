A Real2Sim2Real Machine Learning Pipeline for Wearable Smart Suits for Adults and Toddlers

Smart garments show strong potential for rehabilitation and posture monitoring, yet most existing systems focus on full-body or lower-limb applications. Upper-body tracking, especially in toddlers, remains underexplored, despite known risks of poor early posture. A major barrier is the reliance on large motion capture datasets, which are time-consuming and difficult to collect. I hypothesize that a real2sim2real machine learning pipeline, translating between motion capture data and sensor readings, will accelerate soft suit development and can be adapted for toddler systems. The first aim focuses on collecting adult motion capture and sensor data to create joint angle arrays, simulate motion in Genesis, and train a regression model to predict the motion capture data. The second aim adapts this pipeline for toddlers using pediatric motion data and scaled modeling. My goal is to create a scalable pipeline that reduces dependence on physical data collection and enables real-time, sensor-driven posture monitoring for both adults and children.

Adaptive Neural Networks for Robust Lung Cancer Risk Prediction with a Focus on Feature Importance and Imputation

The lungs are essential organs responsible for exchanging oxygen and carbon dioxide between the blood and the environment, a process vital for maintaining the body's homeostasis 1. Lung cancer ranks as the second most common cancer in humans, with projections for 2024 indicating over 230,000 new cases and approximately 125,000 fatalities in the United States alone 2. These figures surpass the combined mortality rates of breast, colon, and prostate cancers. Lung cancer occurs when cells in the lungs grow uncontrollably, often due to genetic mutations and exposure to carcinogens like tobacco smoke and environmental pollutants, which cause direct DNA damage and lead to tumor development 3. Additional risk factors include family history and the use of certain dietary supplements 2. Currently, low-dose computed tomography (LDCT), shown in figure 1, is the primary method for lung cancer screening, capturing lung images to detect cancer presence. However, the US Preventive Services Task Force recommends LDCT screening only for adults aged 50 to 80 years with a 20-year smoking history who currently smoke or have quit within the past 15 years 4. This criterion may leave some individuals undetected until it is too late. Additionally, while low-dose computed tomography (LDCT) exposes patients to significantly less radiation than conventional CT scans, the radiation dose of approximately 1.4 mSv is still substantial, given that the average natural exposure to radiation for a person ranges from 3 to 5 mSv annually5. High levels of radiation exposure can elevate the risk of cancer, sterility, hair loss, reduced thyroid function, and radiation burns on the skin6. Balancing the probability of detecting lung cancer and the associated costs and risks of LDCT screenings presents a significant challenge. From 2016 to 2020, one-fourth of diagnosed lung cancers were found at the local stage with a 5-year relative survival rate of 58%; another fourth were found at the regional stage, with a survival rate of 33%; while almost half were diagnosed at the distant stage, where the survival rate drops to 8% 7. The National Lung Screening Trial (NLST) revealed that LDCT had an average false-positive rate of 23.3% per screening round, leading to high costs and repeated low-dose radiation exposure, which increases cancer risk 8. In our modern society, machine learning and artificial intelligence (AI) have become crucial for advancing healthcare technology. AI enables computers to mimic human cognitive functions, using math and logic to simulate reasoning similar to human decision-making 9. Machine learning, a subset of AI, involves teaching computers to recognize patterns in data and apply this understanding to new tasks 9. Currently, 22% of firms are pursuing AI integration, 33% are engaging in limited implementation, and 45% are still exploring its use, reflecting its significant growth 10. Machine learning is extensively explored in healthcare for applications such as cancer detection (including skin, breast, and brain cancers, etc) 11, medical imaging 12, personalized treatment plans 13, and drug responses 14. Regarding lung cancer prediction, we propose a method to balance the trade-offs of lung cancer screening guidelines and reduce false-positive rates by utilizing machine learning to predict lung cancer risk based on personal health data. Previous studies have shown promise in this area. Hart et al. demonstrated the potential of using an artificial neural network to predict lung cancer risk with an AUC of approximately 0.86 using NHIS adult survey files from the CDC website 15. Additionally, Chandran et al. developed a high-dimensional model using least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO model) trained on Optum’s de-identified Electronic Health Record dataset, achieving an AUC of 0.76 16. Despite the abundance of research on using machine learning models to predict lung cancer risk, our paper focuses on the practical application of implementing such models into an electronic health record system. Specifically, we address the challenge of missing data when using neural networks in a clinical setting. We developed two models: the "feature rich" model, which uses a variety of personal health data features to predict lung cancer, and the "feature streamlined" model, which examines subsets of features to identify the best imputation methods for accurate predictions. Our goal is to determine feature importance and optimize the questions doctors should ask to enhance the model's performance.
